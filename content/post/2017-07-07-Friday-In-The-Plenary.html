R Consortium sponsor talk

The R Consortium is a not-for-profit organisation which funds most of the key infrastructure for the R Project. Sponsors include IBM, Microsoft, Google, RStudio as well as several tech firms and data consultancies. They exist as a group of business enterprises interested in R thriving in the long-term. In essence a way for businesses who use R to contribute back. They see themselves as the link between the R Foundation, Industry and the R Community.

Turns out the R Consortium calls themselves “a Linux Foundation project”.

In terms of what the Consortium does:

  • one of the first projects of the Consortium was creating R-Hub to help developers and this is described as a major motivator for the consortium as a part of their future proofing efforts.

  • the Infrastructure Steering Committee headed by Hadley

  • working groups are set up for future proofs, a code coverage tool and certification

  • There is an annual R community survey!

  • Funding for what they also call “infrastructure” involving conferences and local useR groups which are all eligible for grants

Finally, David Smith, the Aussie at Microsoft, said some very nice things as a board member at the consortium (all of the major sponsors get a seat on the board). Microsoft sees a huge amount of value in the ecosystem of R. “Microsoft doesn’t do anything out of the goodness of their heart, if it has one.”

20 Years of CRAN

Uwe Ligges has been the head of CRAN since 2003. He describes CRAN (Comprehensive R Archive Network) simply as a standardised way of adding packages to software. This presentation involved a lot of love and patting each other on the back.

In 1997 CRAN was created for the R Project to mirror what was already in existence for Pearl (CPAN) and LaTeX (CTAN). There was much discussion later about how CRAN had become the most successful archive network there is. In 2007 S, as in the software R was based on, tried to introduce CSAN and went nowhere with it. David Smith described CRAN as the most important element of the success of R.

There was also some updates on the current state of CRAN. Currently there are \(2^{13}\) packages on CRAN. He then listed the packages with the most dependencies on other packages with the clear winner being knitr with 2,161 (counting dependencies within dependencies).

A bit of practical advice was to test your package without RStudio or devtools before submitting and generally try to minimise the number of dependencies you need.. because packages can update and break things.

Now the question on a few people’s lips: what keeps CRAN free of malware?

  1. there are no guarantees.. this is the nature of opensource software

  2. there are many sets of eyes on any package at any time. i.e. herd immunity

  3. no binaries can be changed with any additional R package because all the binaries on CRAN were built on CRAN! Thus packages can only code what CRAN lets it CRAN code in the R software.
